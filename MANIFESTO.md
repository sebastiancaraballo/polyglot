# The Polyglot Manifesto

> Why Polyglot exists, and the principles that guide it.

## A language is two things at once

Every language is bound to a culture — its values, rituals, humor, and ways of seeing the
world. And every language exists to express what is universally human: to greet, to ask,
to thank, to want, to fear, to hope, to remember, to say goodbye.

Polyglot holds both truths at once. Under every course is a **universal spine** — the
human functions and emotions shared by all languages — and over it a **cultural skin**
that makes learning a language feel like stepping into the world that speaks it. Adding a
language is dressing a new skin over a skeleton that already exists, not building a course
from scratch.

## Learning a language is living a story

Isolated drills don't add up to a journey. Polyglot's tools are mechanics inside **one
story**: you arrive somewhere, you learn to read the world around you, you gather words
like memories, and you use them with people. The narrative decides *what you meet and why
you care*; spaced practice works underneath to make it *stick*. Progress is earned through
**mastery**, not by clicking "next".

Stories are set in **real places**. Their characters are **invented but culturally
emblematic and dignified — never caricatures** — chosen to show the best of a culture
while you learn its language.

## Your native language is the lens

There is no universal "beginner". The distance between any two languages is different —
different sounds, scripts, false friends, and bridges — so the same starting point feels
different depending on where you begin. Polyglot treats the learner's **native
language(s) as a first-class input** that shapes pacing, scaffolding, contrastive notes,
and which prior knowledge to lean on.

## Decisions are grounded in evidence, not intuition

What to teach, and in what order, follows established standards and word-frequency data,
and the science of how people learn: keep cognitive load low (small pieces before whole
sentences), make decoding automatic before building meaning on it, and use retrieval
practice and spaced repetition. We stand on proven frameworks instead of reinventing the
curriculum.

## Built for everyone, including the overlooked

Most tools serve a handful of profitable languages. The universal spine is exactly what
lets Polyglot reach **low-resource and endangered languages** too: the human core can be
delivered with modest content, capabilities degrade gracefully when audio or data are
scarce, and wrapping each language in its own culture and dignity — not as a lesser cousin
of a bigger one — is part of the point.

## Free and open, all the way down

Polyglot is open source and **MIT-licensed**, and that promise extends to *everything* in
the project — not just the code, but the lessons, word lists, fonts, audio, and images. We
use only freely licensed, compatible resources, attribute what we build on, and follow
open-source best practices so the project stays welcoming and reusable.
